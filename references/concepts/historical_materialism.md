# Historical Materialism

## Working definition
Historical materialism is best used here as a claim that social life is shaped, constrained, and patterned by material production, reproduction, technology, institutions, and the relations built around them.

For this package, it should be read as:
**history under material constraints, not a rigid one-line stage theory.**

---

## Plain-language translation
- path dependence under material constraints
- institutions shaped by production and reproduction
- present arrangements are historical, not natural

---

## Strong use
- explaining why a system became normal
- tracing path dependence
- showing how institutions outlive their original conditions
- connecting economic and non-economic structures without crude reduction

---

## Common mistakes
- reading it as “economy explains everything”
- treating base/superstructure as a simple one-way machine
- assuming all societies must pass through the same stages
- turning broad tendencies into precise predictions

---

## Mini protocol
1. Identify the material and institutional conditions.
2. Trace the historical path by which the arrangement emerged.
3. Show what keeps it stable.
4. Specify which non-economic factors are relatively autonomous.
5. Mark what is contingent, not inevitable.

---

## Source-note orientation
- Canonical support: Marx’s 1859 *Preface* and related texts
- Scholarly caution: interpretations differ sharply on explanatory scope, the role of class struggle, and how strongly non-economic structures are determined
- Generalization note: in modern analytical use, this often functions best as historically informed institutional analysis
