# Freedom and Necessity

## Core idea
Freedom is not the absence of conditions.
In serious analysis, freedom means the capacity to act intelligently **within and upon necessity**.

Necessity names the constraints, dependencies, and non-negotiable conditions shaping the field of action.
Freedom names the real room for maneuver, recombination, and intervention inside that field.

This category prevents both voluntarism and fatalism.

---

## What mistake it corrects
It corrects two common failures:
- “People just need to choose differently.”
- “Nothing can be done because the structure is too strong.”

The proper question is:
**What constraints are real, and what forms of agency remain possible within them?**

---

## Runtime use
Use this category when:
- the user wants strategy under hard constraints
- the problem mixes structure and agency
- the answer risks blaming individuals for system effects
- the answer risks turning structural diagnosis into passivity

---

## Control questions
1. What necessities constrain the case?
2. Which necessities are short-term, and which are deeper?
3. What options remain genuinely open?
4. Which action changes the field of necessity itself?
5. What kind of freedom is realistic here: tactical, strategic, or transformative?

---

## Example
A worker in a weak labor market cannot simply “opt out” of dependence.
That necessity is real.
But there may still be freedom at other levels: skill concentration, coalition-building, employer selection, timing, or institutional reform.

---

## Good output move
> “The useful question is not whether the actor is fully free, but what degrees and levels of freedom exist within the present necessity, and which actions can widen them over time.”

---

## Warning
Do not use this category as moral consolation.
Its value is analytical: it identifies where action remains possible without denying structural limits.
