# Quantity and Quality

## Core idea
Small changes do not always remain small.
Accumulation can eventually alter the form, function, or character of a system.

This category tracks the relation between:
- **quantitative change**: more or less of something
- **qualitative change**: a change in kind, not just degree

---

## Why this matters
Many weak analyses miss thresholds.

They see:
- slightly more workload
- slightly lower trust
- slightly higher churn
- slightly more policy friction

But they miss the point where incremental change becomes:
- burnout
- legitimacy crisis
- cultural breakdown
- business model transformation
- institutional rupture

---

## Questions to ask
- What is changing gradually?
- Is there a threshold beyond which the system behaves differently?
- Which metric is accumulating without being conceptually reclassified?
- At what point does “more of the same” become “something different”?

---

## Analytical use
Examples:
- modest added coordination cost eventually produces a different organization type
- gradual extraction pressure eventually changes user perception of the whole platform
- repeated minor delays eventually destroy trust rather than merely reduce satisfaction

---

## Rule of use
Do not assume there is always a dramatic threshold.
But always ask whether one exists.

---

## Misuse to avoid
### 1. Premature rupture claims
Do not announce a qualitative shift too early.

### 2. Pure gradualism
Do not assume all change is smoothly continuous.

### 3. Metric blindness
Do not keep tracking accumulation without checking whether the underlying object has changed in kind.

---

## Best fit
Use when:
- slow accumulation is being underestimated
- there may be a tipping point
- people think current incremental drift is harmless

---

## Plain-language translation
- quantity -> incremental accumulation
- quality -> change in kind / threshold shift
