# Essence and Appearance

## Core idea
What appears on the surface may be real, but incomplete.
The visible form of a problem is not always the mechanism that reproduces it.

This category does **not** license mystical hiddenness.
It asks for a disciplined distinction between:
- what is immediately visible,
- and what underlying relations generate that appearance.

---

## Why this matters
Many answers stop too early:
- low engagement
- poor morale
- bad culture
- weak execution
- consumer preference
- market demand

These may be valid appearances.
But the analysis improves when it asks:
What structure or process makes these appearances recur?

---

## Questions to ask
- What is visible here?
- What mechanism would generate exactly this pattern?
- What is being measured, and what falls outside the measure?
- Does the official description name the symptom but miss the generator?

---

## Analytical use
Weak:
> “Workers are disengaged.”

Stronger:
> “Disengagement appears as an attitude problem, but is being generated by low control, fragmented feedback, and weak connection between effort and meaningful outcome.”

---

## Discipline rule
Do not oppose appearance and essence as if one is fake and the other is magically true.
The surface matters.
The point is to explain it, not dismiss it.

---

## Misuse to avoid
### 1. Hidden-depth theater
Do not assume the “real cause” is always deeper, grander, or more ideological.

### 2. Pure debunking
Do not treat all surface reports as false consciousness.

### 3. Evidence-free mechanism claims
If you name an underlying mechanism, show why it fits the visible pattern.

---

## Best fit
Use when:
- the symptom is obvious but the cause is unclear
- official metrics distort the picture
- a widely accepted framing seems shallow

---

## Plain-language translation
- appearance -> surface pattern
- essence -> generative mechanism / underlying relation
