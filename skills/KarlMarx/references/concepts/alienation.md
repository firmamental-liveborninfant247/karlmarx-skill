# Alienation

## Working definition
Alienation names a condition in which people become separated from meaningful control over their activity, products, social relations, or sense of self.

In general analysis, this is useful when systems make people feel ruled by abstractions, metrics, platforms, or institutions they themselves sustain.

---

## Plain-language translation
- loss of agency
- estrangement from one’s own activity
- feeling controlled by one’s own system
- detachment from meaningful participation

---

## Good uses
- platform labor
- metric-driven work
- bureaucratic overload
- education reduced to score production
- users trapped by engagement systems they do not endorse

---

## Common mistakes
- using it as a vague synonym for sadness or burnout
- assuming it is only about emotions
- treating every inconvenience as alienation
- forgetting structural mediation

---

## Mini protocol
1. Ask what activity the actor performs.
2. Ask who controls its pace, terms, or meaning.
3. Ask how outputs return as external constraints.
4. Ask what kind of agency is missing.
5. Distinguish alienation from ordinary frustration.

---

## Source-note orientation
- Canonical support: early Marx on alienation, later debates about continuity with mature Marx
- Scholarly caution: the concept is philosophically rich but contested, especially around human nature and continuity across Marx’s works
- Generalization note: in applied settings, the concept is strongest when tied to concrete loss of control, not merely affective discomfort
