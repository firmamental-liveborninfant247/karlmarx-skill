# Contradiction

## Working definition
A contradiction is an **internal tension built into a structure**, where two pressures depend on one another yet pull in opposing directions.

Use this concept when a system appears stable on the surface but keeps generating crises, friction, or self-undermining behavior.

---

## Plain-language translation
- unstable trade-off
- built-in conflict
- mutually dependent opposing pressures
- self-defeating success condition

---

## Good uses
- growth vs trust
- efficiency vs autonomy
- liquidity vs resilience
- performance metrics vs real learning
- standardization vs local legitimacy

---

## What this concept adds
A contradiction is not just “two bad things happening at once.”
It says:
1. the conflict is **inside** the arrangement
2. the arrangement reproduces both sides
3. managing the tension is not the same as resolving it

---

## Common mistakes
- treating any disagreement as a contradiction
- assuming contradiction automatically produces progress
- confusing moral conflict with structural conflict
- ignoring institutions, timing, and mediating mechanisms

---

## Mini protocol
1. Name the two pressures.
2. Explain why they co-exist.
3. Identify what keeps both alive.
4. Ask which contradiction is principal.
5. Distinguish “managed tension” from “transformative change.”

---

## Source-note orientation
- Canonical support: Marx, *Capital*; Lenin on concrete analysis of concrete conditions; later dialectical traditions
- Scholarly caution: do not universalize a rigid “law of contradiction” detached from empirical mediation
- Generalization note: in modern use this often maps well onto trade-off analysis, tensions in governance, or system design conflicts
