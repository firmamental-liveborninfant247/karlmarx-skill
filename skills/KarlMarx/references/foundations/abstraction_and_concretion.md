# Abstraction and Concretion

## Core idea
Analysis often begins with abstraction: a simplified relation, model, or mechanism isolated for clarity.
But good analysis does not stop there.
It returns to the **concrete**: the structured whole with multiple determinations, asymmetries, frictions, and mediations.

This category is one of the most practical safeguards in the package.
It prevents elegant but thin answers.

---

## What mistake it corrects
It corrects two linked failures:
- premature concreteness: drowning in details without a model
- frozen abstraction: using one clean schema as if it were the full case

The core question is:
**What abstraction is clarifying the case, and what concrete determinations must now be reintroduced?**

---

## Runtime use
Use this category when:
- the answer sounds generic and transferable but not case-sensitive
- a broad concept is doing too much work
- the user needs a concrete diagnosis rather than a theoretical template
- multiple determinations are interacting and must be reassembled

---

## Control questions
1. What simplified relation am I using as an entry point?
2. Which concrete determinations does this abstraction currently leave out?
3. Which omitted determinations matter most in this case?
4. What changes once those determinations are restored?
5. Is the answer still true after returning to the concrete whole?

---

## Example
“Retention problems come from weak motivation” is a thin abstraction.
A more concrete answer reintroduces product pacing, reward timing, social proof, price pressure, user segment differences, and organizational KPI incentives.

---

## Good output move
> “The initial abstraction is useful, but it becomes misleading if treated as the whole explanation. The concrete case adds at least three determinations that change the diagnosis.”

---

## Warning
Do not treat “concrete” as “more anecdotes”.
Concrete means a richer structured whole, not just more local color.
