# Possibility and Actuality

## Core idea
Not everything that is imaginable is a real possibility.
Not every real possibility becomes actual.

A possibility becomes actual only through conditions, sequence, capability, timing, and struggle.

This category prevents two opposite errors:
- fantasy: treating wishes as near-term possibilities
- fatalism: treating the current actual arrangement as the only possible one

---

## What mistake it corrects
It corrects loose talk about “could”, “might”, “must”, or “inevitable”.

The key question is:
**Which possibilities are materially open, which are blocked, and what conditions would convert one into reality?**

---

## Runtime use
Use this category when:
- the user asks about strategy, roadmaps, reform, or transition
- a diagnosis sounds trapped in the present
- a proposal is theoretically attractive but conditionally weak
- the answer needs scenario discipline

---

## Control questions
1. What is merely thinkable here?
2. What is a live possibility under current constraints?
3. What enabling conditions are missing?
4. What would make the possibility more actual and less speculative?
5. Which path is adjacent, and which path would require a deeper rupture?

---

## Example
A team says it wants “fully decentralized decision-making”.
That may be thinkable in principle.
But under current staffing, information systems, accountability rules, and managerial incentives, it may not yet be an actual possibility.
A nearer actual possibility may be partial delegation with transparent escalation rules.

---

## Good output move
> “A better distinction here is between what is imaginable in principle and what is actualizable under present conditions. The stronger path is the one that changes the enabling conditions first.”

---

## Warning
Do not collapse this category into bland feasibility talk.
It is not only about practical convenience.
It is about the relation between latent structural possibility and the conditions of realization.
