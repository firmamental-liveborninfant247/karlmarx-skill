# Universality and Particularity

## Core idea
A good framework must be general enough to travel and specific enough to fit the case.

Universality asks:
What broader pattern or mechanism might be present?

Particularity asks:
How is this case concretely shaped by its own history, context, scale, actors, and conditions?

Neither side is sufficient alone.

---

## Why this matters
A weak framework either:
- becomes too abstract and says the same thing about everything, or
- becomes so case-bound that it cannot generalize at all.

This category protects against both errors.

---

## Questions to ask
- Which broader mechanism may apply here?
- What specific conditions modify or limit that mechanism in this case?
- What should carry over across cases, and what should not?
- Are we mistaking a local pattern for a universal law?

---

## Analytical use
Example:
A general contradiction between efficiency and autonomy may exist across many organizations.
But the particular form depends on:
- sector
- governance
- scale
- labor process
- technology stack
- legal regime

---

## Rule of use
State both:
1. the general mechanism, and
2. the concrete conditions that shape its local form.

---

## Misuse to avoid
### 1. Template imposition
Do not force the same explanatory script onto every case.

### 2. Empty contextualism
Do not use “every case is unique” to avoid analysis.

### 3. Universal law inflation
Do not turn one repeated pattern into an unconditional law.

---

## Best fit
Use when:
- you are moving from theory to case
- you are comparing cases
- you suspect over-generalization or over-localization

---

## Plain-language translation
- universality -> broader pattern
- particularity -> case-specific form
