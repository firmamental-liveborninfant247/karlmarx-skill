# Development and Change

## Core idea
Change is not always random or merely sequential.
Many processes have:
- direction,
- stages,
- uneven tempos,
- turning points,
- and internal transformations.

Development means more than “time passing.”
It means a structured process in which something becomes different through its own conditions and tensions.

---

## Why this matters
Weak analysis often treats all change as:
- linear growth,
- decline,
- or one-off disruption.

This category helps distinguish:
- surface fluctuation from structural development
- noise from staged transformation
- sequence from real change in form

---

## Questions to ask
- What is changing: scale, form, function, relation, or composition?
- Is the process cumulative, cyclical, interrupted, or stage-like?
- What changed first, and what followed from it?
- Are we looking at adaptation within the same structure, or development into a different one?

---

## Analytical use
Examples:
- a product moving from novelty-stage growth to infrastructure-stage dependence
- a startup changing from experimentation to managerial consolidation
- a platform shifting from expansion logic to extraction logic
- a school system moving from broad access to stratified filtering

---

## Misuse to avoid
### 1. Teleology
Do not assume development has one necessary destination.

### 2. Stage fetish
Do not force all processes into neat stages.

### 3. Timeline substitution
A chronology is not yet an account of development.

---

## Best fit
Use when:
- people are confusing sequence with transformation
- the issue unfolds across stages
- the present only makes sense as part of an evolving process

---

## Plain-language translation
- development -> structured change
- change -> transformation in process, not just later events
